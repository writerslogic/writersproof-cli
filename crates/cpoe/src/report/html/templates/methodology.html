Competing Hypotheses

H₁ (Proponent Hypothesis)

The submitted document was composed through a human writing process, exhibiting behavioral patterns characteristic of natural cognitive composition, including variable keystroke timing, iterative revision, and organic pause structures.

H₂ (Alternative Hypothesis)

The submitted document was generated or substantially produced by automated means (including but not limited to large language models), or entered by transcription, dictation, or paste from an external source.

Examination Procedure

This examination was conducted using the Cryptographic Proof of Effort (CPoE) protocol, an automated forensic system that captures behavioral telemetry during document creation. The system records keystroke dynamics, inter-keystroke timing intervals (IKI; the time gap between consecutive key presses), revision patterns, application focus events, and cursor movements in real time. These observations are cryptographically bound to sequential checkpoints using Proof of Sequential Memory-bound Effort (PoSME; a memory-hard sequential computation resistant to GPU/ASIC acceleration), producing a tamper-evident evidentiary chain that can be independently verified by any party.

The assessment score is derived from a multi-dimensional analysis comparing observed writing patterns against reference distributions for human-authored and machine-generated text. Each analytical dimension produces an independent likelihood ratio (LR; the ratio of the probability of observing the evidence under H₁ to the probability under H₂). The per-dimension LRs are combined by logarithmic summation with cross-correlation weighting applied to correlated dimensions to avoid double-counting evidence. The composite LR is classified on the ENFSI (European Network of Forensic Science Institutes) verbal equivalence scale. This approach follows the framework described in the ENFSI Guideline for Evaluative Reporting in Forensic Science (2015).
